# Sport-Specific Adaptations

## Endurance Athletes (Runners, Cyclists, Triathletes, Swimmers)

- Aerobic base is the foundation. Z2 volume is never wasted. See [[intensity-zones#Polarized Training Model (Endurance)]].
- Threshold development (FTP, LTHR, CSS) is the primary performance driver for most amateurs.
- Long sessions build durability. Do not replace a 3-hour ride with 2x 1.5-hour rides and expect the same adaptation.
- Brick sessions (bike-to-run, swim-to-bike) for multisport athletes: introduce only after base phase. Start with easy transitions, progress to race-pace transitions.

## Strength Athletes (Bodybuilders, Powerlifters, General Strength)

- Progressive overload via weight, reps, or sets — not all three simultaneously.
- RPE 7-8 for working sets in a build phase. RPE 9-10 reserved for testing or peaking. See [[intensity-zones#RPE Scale (Strength)]].
- Compound movements first, isolation second. Prioritize movement quality over load.
- Deload week: reduce volume 40-50% (fewer sets), maintain intensity (same weight, fewer reps). See [[periodization#Mesocycle Structure]].
- Hypertrophy: 10-20 sets per muscle group per week. Strength: 3-6 reps, heavier loads, more rest. See [[thresholds#Intensity Distribution]].

## Hybrid Athletes (CrossFit, OCR, Triathlon + Strength)

- Interference effect is real. Concurrent endurance and strength training requires careful session ordering and recovery management.
- Sequence: strength before endurance on the same day (protect neural quality for strength).
- Separate high-intensity endurance and heavy strength by at least 6 hours, ideally 24. See [[thresholds#Session Spacing]].
- Accept that neither modality will progress as fast as in a single-sport program. This is a tradeoff, not a failure.
