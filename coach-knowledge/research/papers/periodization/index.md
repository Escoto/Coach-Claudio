# Periodization Research Evidence

> **Bottom line:** Progressive loading within 10% weekly caps and 3:1/4:1 mesocycles is supported by coaching consensus and the ACWR injury model. The specific 10% number has weak direct evidence — the real guardrail is avoiding spikes >30% while maintaining adequate chronic load.

## Key Findings

### 10% Weekly Volume Rule
- Systematic review of 31 studies found **conflicting results** on volume-injury relationships; impossible to identify which training errors cause running injuries -- (Nielsen et al., 2012)
- Runners increasing weekly distance **>30% had HR 1.59** (95% CI: 0.96-2.66) vs <10% increase. No difference between 10% and 24% increase (HR=0.8, 95% CI: 0.6-1.3). Very limited evidence overall (only 4 studies met criteria) -- (Damsted et al., 2018)
- **Applied as:** [[thresholds#Load Progression]] max weekly volume increase of +10% — conservative proxy supported by Damsted's <10% safe zone

### Training-Injury Prevention Paradox
- Higher chronic workloads are **protective** against injury. Non-contact injuries caused by inappropriate programming (rapid load spikes), not training per se. ACWR is the best practice predictor of training-related injuries -- (Gabbett, 2016)
- Marathon runners <30 km/week had **RR 2.02** (95% CI: 1.26-3.24) for injury vs 30-60 km/week. No significant difference between >60 km/week and 30-60 km/week (RR=1.13, p=0.80) — under-training is itself a risk factor -- (Rasmussen et al., 2013)
- ACWR ≥1.3 in juniors and ≥1.5 in adults identified as **strongest extrinsic injury predictor** in tennis. 4-week ramp-up strategies reduced injury incidence by 21%; core stability + eccentric rotator cuff training reduced overuse by 26% -- (Amor-Salamanca et al., 2025)
- **Applied as:** [[thresholds#Workload Ratio]] ACWR 0.8-1.3 optimal; the coach enforces progression caps AND ensures adequate chronic load

### Deload Protocols
- Delphi consensus (34 coaches, 3 rounds): deloading is "a period of reduced training stress designed to mitigate physiological and psychological fatigue, promote recovery, and enhance preparedness." Ubiquitous in practice yet **under-researched**. Continuous challenging training without enough recovery can lead to NFOR or OTS. Tapering in strength sports: ~7 days, volume reduction while maintaining/slightly reducing intensity -- (Bell et al., 2023)
- **Applied as:** [[thresholds#Load Progression]] deload volume reduction 40-50%, maintain intensity; [[periodization#Mesocycle Structure]]

### Periodized Carbohydrate Restriction
- Meta-analysis of 9 studies in trained athletes (VO2max ≥55/60 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹): periodized CHO restriction showed **no overall performance effect** vs normal high-CHO training (SMD = 0.17, P = 0.29). Chronic LCHF diets impair high-intensity capacity. However, acute exercise with low glycogen does stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis -- (Gejl & Nybo, 2021)
- **Applied as:** [[nutrition#Fueling Strategy]] train-low sessions may have adaptive value but do not replace high-CHO availability for competition and high-intensity work
