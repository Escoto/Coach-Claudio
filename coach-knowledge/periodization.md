# Periodization & Programming

## Progressive Overload

The goal is consistent forward loading without spikes. The body adapts to gradually increasing demands — but only when the increase is small enough to trigger adaptation rather than breakdown.

- Max **+10% weekly volume increase** (duration or distance) over previous week. See [[thresholds#Load Progression]].
- This 10% cap is a conservative proxy. The real danger zone is **>30% week-over-week increase**, where injury risk rises sharply. Increases of 10-24% show no significantly different injury risk from <10% — but we default to the tighter cap because athletes rarely need faster progression and the cost of injury far exceeds the cost of patience.
- After a deload week, resume at the volume of the **last full training week** — not the deload week. Using the deload as baseline would artificially inflate the allowed increase.
- If last week showed high fatigue, missed sessions, or declining readiness: **maintain or slightly reduce** load. Never play "catch-up" after a bad week — the training debt is already paid in fatigue; adding more volume compounds the problem.

## The Training-Injury Prevention Paradox

Under-training is a risk factor, not just over-training. Athletes with higher chronic training loads sustain **fewer** injuries than those with low chronic loads — provided they built that load progressively. The paradox:

- **Reducing load is not always protective.** Athletes accustomed to high workloads are more resilient. The instinct to "rest more" after minor niggles can erode the chronic load base that was protecting them in the first place.
- **Spikes cause injuries, not load itself.** Non-contact soft-tissue injuries are driven by rapid increases in acute load relative to what the body is prepared for. The ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio) captures this: stay in the **0.8-1.3 range** for safety. See [[thresholds#Workload Ratio]].
- **Minimum volume matters.** Marathon runners training below 30 km/week face roughly double the injury risk of those training 30-60 km/week. Above 60 km/week offers no additional protection but also no additional risk — the protective effect plateaus rather than reverses.

Practical implication: when an athlete returns from illness, travel, or a down period, the priority is **rebuilding chronic load progressively**, not protecting them from work. A 4-week ramp-up back to previous loads, respecting the 10% weekly cap, is safer than extended light training that leaves them under-prepared.

## Mesocycle Structure

- **Standard:** 3 weeks build, 1 week deload (3:1). Suitable for most athletes.
- **Advanced:** 4 weeks build, 1 week deload (4:1). For experienced athletes with established recovery habits and monitoring. Requires readiness tracking to confirm the athlete tolerates the longer build.
- Recovery weeks are **mandatory, not optional**. Continuous challenging training without planned recovery drives athletes from productive overreaching toward non-functional overreaching (NFOR) or overtraining syndrome (OTS). The line between these states is only visible in hindsight — plan deloads proactively rather than waiting for breakdown.

### Build Weeks
Each build week should increase volume within the +10% cap while intensity distribution stays roughly constant. The stimulus is cumulative — three progressively harder weeks create a manageable fatigue debt that the deload repays.

- Week 1: baseline volume, normal intensity
- Week 2: +5-10% volume
- Week 3: +5-10% volume over Week 2 (peak week)
- Week 4: deload (see below)

### Block Periodization
For athletes with specific competition targets, organize mesocycles into blocks:

- **Accumulation:** High volume, moderate intensity. Build aerobic base (endurance) or work capacity (strength). 2-3 mesocycles.
- **Transmutation:** Moderate volume, higher intensity. Convert base fitness into sport-specific output. 1-2 mesocycles.
- **Realization:** Reduced volume, competition-specific intensity. Taper and peak. 1 mesocycle.

This structure avoids the limitation of mixed training — trying to develop everything simultaneously produces conflicting signals and insufficient stimulus for any single quality.

## Deload Programming

Deloading is a period of reduced training stress designed to dissipate accumulated fatigue while preserving fitness. It is not rest — it is strategic under-loading.

### Volume and Intensity
- **Reduce volume by 40-50%**, maintain intensity. Cutting intensity instead is less effective — the neuromuscular stimulus needs to be preserved; it's the accumulated volume that drives fatigue.
- For strength athletes: reduce working sets by ~50%, keep loads at or near training weights. A deload is not a light week of high reps — it's fewer sets at meaningful loads.
- For endurance athletes: cut total duration/distance by 40-50%, keep key sessions (e.g., one threshold session) but shorten them.

### Planned vs Reactive
- **Planned deloads** (every 3rd or 4th week) are the default. They work because fatigue accumulates predictably in structured programs.
- **Reactive deloads** trigger when readiness metrics decline: 2+ consecutive poor sleep nights, resting HR elevated ≥5 bpm for 2+ days, 3 consecutive low HRV readings, or subjective readiness consistently <60. See [[thresholds#Recovery & Readiness]].
- If reactive signals fire mid-build (e.g., Week 2 of a 3:1 cycle), take the deload early and restart the build cycle. Do not try to "finish the block" through deteriorating readiness.

### Strength Sport Taper
For powerlifting, weightlifting, or strength-focused peaking: the final deload before competition is a taper of approximately 7 days. Reduce volume while maintaining or slightly reducing intensity. The final training session should be 3-5 days before competition day.

## Intensity Distribution

- **Endurance athletes:** 80/20 polarized — 80% below VT1/Z2, 20% above VT2/threshold. The middle zone (tempo/Z3) is used sparingly and with specific purpose (e.g., race-pace practice, lactate clearance work). See [[intensity-zones]].
- **Strength athletes:** RPE-based autoregulation. Programmed intensity (% of 1RM or RPE target) with built-in adjustment based on daily readiness. Build phase RPE 7-8; test/peak RPE 9-10 only.
- **Hybrid athletes:** Prioritize the limiting factor. If aerobic base is weak, endurance volume takes priority. If strength is the limiter, protect strength sessions. See [[sport-adaptations]].

## Periodized Nutrition

Nutritional periodization should align with training periodization. Two key principles:

- **High carbohydrate availability for competition and high-intensity sessions.** There is no performance benefit to chronic carbohydrate restriction in well-trained endurance athletes. Periodized CHO restriction (train-low) does not improve performance over normal high-CHO training.
- **Selective train-low sessions** may have adaptive value — training with low muscle glycogen stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis. But this is a supplementary tool for low-intensity aerobic sessions only, never for threshold work, intervals, or competition preparation. See [[nutrition#Fueling Strategy]].

## Session Sequencing

- **Hard day / easy day alternation** as default.
- Minimum **48 hours** between threshold or VO2max sessions.
- Strength before endurance on the same day (if doubling) — unless the endurance session is the priority.
- Swim before upper body strength (not after — fatigued shoulders increase injury risk).
- For hybrid athletes: separate high-intensity endurance and heavy strength by at least **6 hours, ideally 24**. See [[thresholds#Session Spacing]].
- When introducing new movements, surfaces, or modalities: start at **50% expected volume** and progress over 2-3 weeks. Novel stimuli carry disproportionate injury risk even at moderate loads.

## Readiness Gates

Every prescribed session must include a readiness gate. See [[thresholds#Recovery & Readiness]] for numerical bands.

- **Hard sessions:** "Gate: if readiness < 60 (or subjective < 3/5), replace with [specific easy alternative]."
- **Easy sessions:** "Gate: if readiness < 40, take a full rest day."
- **Below 40 on any scale:** rest. No exceptions.

The alternative must be specific — not "take it easy" but "30 min Z1 spin" or "20 min easy walk."

## Post-Spike Recovery

After races, max-effort testing, or any unplanned training spike:

- Injury risk remains elevated for **7-14 days**. See [[thresholds#Session Spacing]].
- The first week back should be at or below deload volume, regardless of how the athlete "feels." Perceived readiness recovers faster than tissue tolerance.
- Return to full programming over 2 weeks, using the spike as the new "acute" load reference for ACWR calculations.
